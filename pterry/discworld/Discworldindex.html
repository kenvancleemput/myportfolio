A look at the Discworld, fantasy's second largest book series

The Discworld in our world

What started as a parody of post-war fantasy grew to be one of the largest (in word count) series to be published in the last 50 years. During the times it was being written, this changed to become a satirical look on many modern institutions. Due to Sir Terry's voracious reading, there were many historical facts interweaved with the narrative. Showing a keen insight into the human spirit, Sir Terry never forgot that at the heart of any novel are the people, and the journey they must make through the fantastical events.

The Discworld

Floating through space is the great turtle Ah'tuin. Carrying four large elephants on its back (who also have names), it swims ever forward through the primordial muck. On the backs of the elephants, we find the Discworld. Inspired by the saying that it's turtles all the way down, we find a fantasy world inspired by J.R.R. Tolkien and sword and sorcery tropes from the postwar era. It has very specific rules, like narrativia, and an new colour called octarine. In the beginning, we followed the adventures of Rincewind, a wizzzard and his client, the tourist Twoflower, as they travel around the disc. A recurring character in nearly every book is DEATH, the anthropomorphic representation of death, and a parody of the grim reaper. Later novels even featured DEATH as a main character, and over the course of 41 novels many recurring characters are introduced.

Themes

In Discworld, there are a few recurring themes. There's the implementation of modern topics in a fantasy world (e.g. popular music, movies, the financial system), a series of whodunit novels, a satirical look at fantastical fiction tropes (vampires, tooth fairies, christmas) etc. A strong preference for humanism can be found in nearly all the novels, and in the Tiffany Aching books, which are aimed at younger readers, a focus on developing critical thinking skills. Due to the broad ranging of topics, I do believe that there is a Discworld book for nearly everyone. They are also the perfect read for when you want to relax, as Sir Terry has the gift to explain difficult concepts in both a humourous and simple way that never demand too much from the reader.
